Vigenère Cipher: A Treasure of Cryptography

Introduction to Vigenère Cipher

The Vigenère cipher is a polyalphabetic substitution cipher that uses a keyword to encrypt plaintext. It was considered unbreakable for centuries and is often called the "le chiffre indéchiffrable" (the indecipherable cipher).

Historical Context

Although named after Blaise de Vigenère, who described it in 1586, the cipher was actually invented by Giovan Battista Bellaso in 1553. It gained popularity during the American Civil War and remained in use well into the 20th century.

How Vigenère Cipher Works

The Vigenère cipher uses a series of interwoven Caesar ciphers based on the letters of a keyword. Each letter of the keyword determines the shift amount for the corresponding letter in the plaintext.

In vigenere cipher we have plain text and a key. Here key is a word and each alphabet is repeated till it covers each alphabet of plaintext.

Strengths and Weaknesses

Strengths:

  • Much stronger than simple substitution ciphers
  • Resistant to frequency analysis
  • Large key space compared to Caesar cipher

Weaknesses:

  • Vulnerable to known-plaintext attacks
  • Key length can be determined through statistical analysis
  • Repeating key pattern can be exploited

Historical Uses of Vigenère Cipher

The Vigenère cipher was used extensively by Confederate forces during the American Civil War. It was also employed by various secret societies and in diplomatic communications throughout the 19th century.

Breaking the Vigenère Cipher

The Vigenère cipher can be broken using several methods, including:

  • Kasiski examination (finding repeated sequences)
  • Friedman test (calculating the index of coincidence)
  • Frequency analysis (once the key length is known)
